Introduction to Quantitative Methods

Question 5

Consider a function \(f: \mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) as follows. \[\begin{align} f(x_1, x_2, x_3) & = \begin{cases} \frac{x_1 x_2 + x_3 ^2 }{x_1^2 + x_2^2 + 2x_3^2}, & \quad \mbox{if } (x_1, x_2, x_3) \neq (1,1,1) \\ 0, & \quad \mbox{if } (x_1, x_2, x_3) = (1,1,1). \end{cases} \end{align}\]

  1. (6%) Show that \(f\) is not continuous at \((1,1,1)\).

  2. (6%) Show that \(\frac{\partial f(x_1, x_2, x_3)}{\partial x_3}\) does not exist at \((1, 1, 1)\).

Solution (a)

(2%) Consider a sequence \(\{x_n\}\) with \[\begin{align} x_{in} = 1 + \frac{1}{n} \end{align}\] where \(x_n = (x_{1n}, x_{2n}, x_{3n})\) and \(i=1,2,3\).

(2%) We know \[\begin{align} x_n \rightarrow (1,1,1) \end{align}\] as \(n \rightarrow \infty\).

(2%) We find \[\begin{align} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} f(x_n) & = \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \frac{2\left( 1+\frac{1}{n} \right)^2}{4\left( 1+\frac{1}{n} \right)^2} = \frac{1}{2} \\ & \neq f(1,1,1) = 0. \end{align}\] Thus, \(f\) is not continuous at \((1,1,1)\).

Solution (b)

(6%) We find \[\begin{align} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_3}(1,1,1) & = \lim_{h\rightarrow 0} \frac{f(1,1,1+h) - f(1,1,1)}{h} \\ & = \lim_{h\rightarrow 0} \frac{\frac{1+(1+h)^2}{1+1+2(1+h)^2} - 0}{h} \\ & = \lim_{h\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{2h} = \infty. \end{align}\] Thus, \(\frac{\partial f(x_1, x_2, x_3)}{\partial x_3}\) does not exist at \((1, 1, 1)\).
