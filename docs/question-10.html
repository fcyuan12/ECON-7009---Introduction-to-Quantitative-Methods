Question 10

Discuss why the following proofs are incorrect.

  1. (5%) Prove that is a sequence has only one limit point \(p\), then this sequence converges.

Proof. Letâ€™s prove by contradiction. Suppose that the sequence \(x_n\) does not converge to \(p\). Then there exists an \(\varepsilon > 0\) and for all \(N\) such that \(d(x_n, p) \geq \varepsilon\) if \(n\geq N\). Then we find an \(\varepsilon\) that there are finite indices \(n\) for which \(d(x_n, p) < \varepsilon\). Therefore, \(p\) cannot be a limit point.

  1. (5%) Suppose that \(x_n\rightarrow 0\). Show that \[\begin{align} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} x_n \sin\frac{1}{x_n} = 0. \end{align}\]

Proof. By \[\begin{align} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_nb_n & = \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} a_n \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} b_n, \end{align}\] we have \[\begin{align} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} x_n \sin\frac{1}{x_n} & = \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} x_n \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \sin\frac{1}{x_n}. \end{align}\] Because the range of \(\sin \frac{1}{x_n}\) is bounded in \([-1,1]\), and \(x_n\) tends to zero as \(n\) sufficiently large, the product of the two must eventually tend to zero.

Related Definition or Theorem

Theorem 1 Suppose \(\{s_n\}, \{t_n\}\) are complex sequences, and \[\begin{align} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} s_n & = s \\ \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} t_n & = t. \end{align}\] Then, \[\begin{align} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} s_n t_n = st. \end{align}\]

(Rudin and others 1964, 3:49 Theorem 3.3 (c))

Solution (a)

(5%) The statement

Then there exists an \(\varepsilon > 0\) and for all \(N\) such that \(d(x_n, p) \geq \varepsilon\) if \(n\geq N\).

is wrong. The correct statement is

Then there exists an \(\varepsilon > 0\) and for all \(N\) such that there exists \(n\geq N\), \[\begin{align} d(x_n, p) \geq \varepsilon. \end{align}\]

Solution (b)

(5%) Because we are not sure whether \[\begin{align} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \sin\frac{1}{x_n} \end{align}\] exists, we cannot use Theorem 1.
