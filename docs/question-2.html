Introduction to Quantitative Methods

Question 2

Consider a sequence \(\{x_n\}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^m\) converges to \(x\).

  1. (8%) Show that every subsequence of \(\{x_n\}\) converges.

  2. (6%) Explain why a subsequence of \(\{x_n\}\) cannot converge to a point different from \(x\).

Solution (a)

(3%) Suppose that \[\begin{align} x_n \rightarrow x \end{align}\] as \(n\rightarrow \infty\). Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), there is \(N\in \mathbb{N}\) such that \[\begin{align} d(x_n, x) < \varepsilon \end{align}\] if \(n \geq N\).

(1%) Let \(\{x_{k(n)}\}\) be any subsequence of \(\{x_n\}\).

(2%) We can find \[\begin{align} x_{k(n)} = x_m \end{align}\] for some \(m\geq n\geq N\).

(2%) We have \[\begin{align} d(x_{k(n)}, x) & = d(x_m, x) < \varepsilon. \end{align}\] Thus, every subsequence of \(\{x_n\}\) converges to \(x\).

Solution (b)

(2%) Suppose that \(\{x_{k(n)}\}\) converges to \(x\) and to \(y\). Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), there is \(N_x, N_y \in \mathbb{N}\) such that \[\begin{align} k(n)\geq N_x & \quad \Rightarrow \quad d(x_{k(n)}, x) < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \\ k(n)\geq N_y & \quad \Rightarrow \quad d(x_{k(n)}, y) < \frac{\varepsilon}{2}. \end{align}\] (2%) Take \(k(n) \geq N = \max\{ N_x, N_y\}\). We have \[\begin{align} d(x,y) & \leq d(x_{k(n)}, x) + d(x_{k(n)}, y) \\ & < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon. \end{align}\] (2%) Because \(\varepsilon\) is arbitrary, \[\begin{align} d(x,y) = 0. \end{align}\] That is, \(x = y\).
