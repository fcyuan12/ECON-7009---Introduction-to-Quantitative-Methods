Introduction to Quantitative Methods

Question 3

(8%) Consider \(f: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^1\) and \(\nabla f(x) \neq 0\) for all \(x\). Let \(\mathcal{D}\) be an open set in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Show that the solution of \(\max \{ f(x) \mid x\in \mathcal{D} \}\) does not exist.

Related Definition or Theorem

Theorem 4 Suppose \(x^* \in \mbox{int} \mathcal{D} \subset \mathbb{R}^n\) is a local maximum of \(f\) on \(\mathcal{D}\), i.e., there is \(r > 0\) such that \(B(x^*, r) \subset \mathcal{D}\) and \(f(x^*) \geq f(x)\) for all \(x \in B(x^*, r)\). Suppose also that \(f\) is differentiable at \(x^*\). Then, \[\begin{align} Df(x^*) = 0. \end{align}\]

(Sundaram and others 1996, 102 Theorem 4.1)

Solution

(3%) Suppose that there is \(x^*\in D\) such that \[\begin{align} f(x^*) \geq f(y) \end{align}\] for all \(y\in D\) and \(f\) is differentiable at \(x^*\).

(3%) We know

  • \(f\) is differentiable at \(x^*\);
  • \(x^*\) is an interior point;
  • \(x^*\) is an local maximum.

(2%) By Theorem 4, \[\begin{align} \nabla f(x) = 0. \end{align}\] This is a contradiction.
